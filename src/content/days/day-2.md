## About this day

Data is not a neutral input — it is generated by a process, for a purpose, and
that process shapes what questions you can credibly ask. The lead lecture treats
health data as a first-class citizen: the data-generating process, data
collected as a byproduct of care versus designed for research, de-identification
and governance, and the practical mechanics of access. The guest lecture covers
the other half of the universe — patient-generated, wearable, and sensor data.
The mode is **interrogate**: treat your dataset as a suspect.

## Lead lecture outline

**Data: the first-class citizen of every health AI problem.** A working outline
of the lecture's content.

- **The day on the spine.** Today closes the next gap: a problem becomes the data
  that actually exists to attack it.
- **The central claim.** Data is generated by a process, for a purpose — and that
  process shapes what you can credibly ask.
- **The data-generating process.** Data collected as a byproduct of care versus
  data designed for research; what the data was built for constrains what it can
  answer (coding artifacts, documentation patterns, care-process confounding).
- **Provenance and bias.** Who never enters the data; who is under-measured; how
  the gaps become silent model assumptions.
- **Working with health data in practice.** What de-identification does and does
  not protect; the governance regime around protected health information; access
  mechanics — PhysioNet credentialing, data use agreements, institutional review
  — and realistic timelines.
- **Data standards.** Why structure matters; MEDS (the Medical Event Data
  Standard) and the value of a common representation.
- **The two data worlds.** Structured institutional clinical data versus
  patient-generated, wearable, and sensor data — what each provenance does to
  bias and label quality.
- **Interrogating your own dataset.** Where did it come from, who is missing,
  what does it silently encode, and does it support your Day 1 problem?

## What you'll be able to do

- Characterize a data-generating process and what it was built for.
- Anticipate provenance-driven bias before modeling.
- Produce a realistic data access plan.

## Small group & specialty breakout

In **Small Group 1**, each participant describes their data-generating process
and what data exists independent of their AI system; the group's question: *what
bias does that provenance create?*

The **specialty breakout** then runs as a data clinic: the main datasets, access
routes, and shared biases in your domain.

The day closes with **Small Group 2**, where you refine the access plan in light
of the breakout and complete your data specification.

## Project Workbook — Part 2: Data

The data-generating process, what data already exists, your access plan,
anticipated provenance-driven bias, and a gap check: does the available data
actually support your Part 1 problem? If not, re-scope the problem or change the
data plan — and note that here.
